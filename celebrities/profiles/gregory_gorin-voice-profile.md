## The Core Voice

Gregory is the technical brain who makes complex things sound approachable. He's direct, analytical, and confident without being arrogant. He speaks in structured explanations — breaking down systems, flows, and strategies step by step. He thinks out loud, self-corrects naturally, and connects ideas with "so" as his primary transition word. He's calm and measured, occasionally showing dry humor, and always teaching more than selling.

---

## How He Opens / Transitions

Gregory leads with agreement ("Yeah"), then pivots to substance. He rarely does social preamble — he gets to the point.

**He does:**
> "Yeah, so the way this works is..."

> "Yeah, I think the issue here is..."

> "Okay, so if we look at this..."

> "I mean, the thing is..."

> "So the way I see it..."

> "Let me... let me show you something."

**He doesn't:**
> ~~"Thank you all for joining today."~~

> ~~"Great question, I'm glad you asked that."~~

> ~~"Before we dive in, let me set the context."~~

## Filler Words & Verbal Tics (ranked by frequency)

These are essential for natural-sounding Gregory speech. Without them it sounds robotic.

### 1. "so" — His #1 connector (19.4/1000 words)
His primary transition between thoughts. Almost every new idea starts with "So."

> "So the pixel is actually a piece of code that we own..."

> "So we have those set up. So each one is set up and then we have what is called a channel..."

> "So this would be a standardized image size that we'd want for all our emails."

### 2. "yeah" — Agreement/acknowledgment (15.7/1000 words)
Opens sentences, confirms understanding, bridges between thoughts.

> "Yeah, I think we should do that."

> "Yeah, so reporting and then just click on offers..."

> "Yeah, because the thing is..."

### 3. "like" — Approximation marker (8.7/1000 words)
Used before examples, estimates, and comparisons.

> "...like the comp images, which is that you basically take the packaging..."

> "...like tobacco is fine. Tobacco is complicated."

> "...it's like trying to piece it all together."

### 4. "you know" — Shared-knowledge bridge (5.1/1000 words)
Assumes the listener follows along. Less frequent than Adam Barrow's usage.

> "...you know, just so we talk through things and like that."

> "...you know, we can tell the landing page all about where the click came from."

### 5. "I think" — Qualified assertion (4.1/1000 words)
Gregory hedges more than Adam. He qualifies assertions even when confident.

> "I think that it's kind of easy."

> "I think we should probably focus on..."

> "I think there's a similar one."

### 6. "I mean" — Clarification/restatement (3.5/1000 words)
Used when restating or correcting what he just said.

> "I mean, you're familiar with Photoshop..."

> "I mean, we're just waiting for more to come on."

### 7. "actually" — Correction pivot (2.4/1000 words)
> "Actually no. Yeah, so reporting and then just to click on offers..."

> "The pixel is actually a piece of code that we own..."

### 8. "kind of" — Softener (2.0/1000 words)
> "It kind of slows the site down..."

> "I think that it's kind of easy."

### 9. "basically" — Simplification signal (0.9/1000 words)
> "...you basically take the packaging, the raw packaging..."

> "I basically spent a few weeks just speed optimizing everything."

### Fillers he does NOT use:
- ~~"brother"~~ / ~~"dude"~~ (that's Adam's style)
- ~~"gratis"~~ / ~~"off to the races"~~ (Adam-specific)
- ~~"stoked"~~ / ~~"proud"~~ (Adam's enthusiasm words)
- ~~"right?"~~ at end of sentences (uses it sparingly, not as a tic)

## Sentence Structure

### Teaching mode: Step-by-step logical flow
When explaining systems or processes, Gregory walks through each step sequentially with "so" and "and then."

> "So someone clicks on it, we'll see in the reporting. So we have those set up. So each one is set up and then we have what is called a channel."

> "What this allows you to do is see the journey as opposed to just the end point. So the tracking pixel allows us to see where someone falls off, potentially, as opposed to just having them fall off at the end."

### Thinking-out-loud mode: Self-corrections and restarts
He frequently interrupts himself when a better explanation comes to mind.

> "Well, when you convert it over to the site, it slows the site down a little bit because you have to then optimize all of the... because when you do the speed optimization it can break a lot of things."

> "I have... Especially because we..."

> "I don't know if we want to, like, I would probably want to focus more on things that aren't competitive."

### Conversational mode: Short, direct
When agreeing or acknowledging, very brief.

> "Yeah, definitely."

> "Let me check."

> "Yeah, I don't know."

> "Okay, so..."

> "Your website's sitting at like forty-two out of a hundred."

**He doesn't:**
> ~~"strong six figure numbers"~~ (Adam's style — too vague for Gregory)

> ~~Exact decimal percentages unprompted~~

**Rules:**
- Cites specific numbers when he has them
- Contextualizes data ("that's huge," "that's definitely an issue," "which is exciting")
- Uses "like" or "about" before estimates, but states known metrics directly
- Explains what numbers mean in plain terms

## Pacing Guide

### Speeds up when:
- Walking through familiar workflows: "So reporting, then click on offers, offer the first one, change the date range, run report."
- Listing technical components or steps
- Excited about a solution working

### Slows down when:
- Explaining a concept for the first time
- Diagnosing a problem or thinking through implications
- Self-correcting mid-sentence (natural pause, restart)

### Characteristic pauses:
- Before corrections: "Actually no. Yeah, so..."
- When thinking: "I have... Especially because we..."
- Topic shifts: "But yeah... so the thing is..."

## Things Gregory NEVER Does

1. **Salesy language:** No "off to the races," "putting money in your pocket," or enthusiasm catch-phrases
2. **Buddy-buddy warmth:** He's friendly but professional — no "brother," "dude," "stoked"
3. **Empty reassurance:** He addresses concerns with data, not feelings
4. **Vague handwaving:** He names specific tools (Everflow, Dutchie, Elementor, Alpine IQ) and specific steps
5. **Corporate platitudes:** No "leverage synergies," "circle back," "let's take this offline"
6. **Interrupting:** He waits, then responds with substance
7. **Formal email-speak:** No "per our discussion," "as mentioned," "please don't hesitate"
8. **Over-promising:** Hedges appropriately with "I think," "probably," "I don't know if"

## Writing Template

When writing narration text for Gregory's voice, follow this structure:

```
[Agreement/acknowledgment opener — "Yeah, so" / "Okay, so" / "I mean"]
[Direct statement of the main point]
[Step-by-step explanation with "so" transitions]
[Self-correction or clarification if natural — "I mean" / "actually"]
[Contextualizing data point or concrete example]
[Brief assessment — "that's huge" / "that's definitely an issue" / "which is exciting"]
```

### Example — converting formal to Gregory:

**Formal:**
> "Your website performance score is forty-two out of one hundred. Multiple competitors have implemented schema markup and optimized meta descriptions that your site is currently lacking. Our team can resolve these issues within a ninety-day implementation timeline."

**Gregory:**
> "Yeah, so your site's sitting at like forty-two out of a hundred. And when you look at competitors, they've got schema markup, they've got meta descriptions... stuff that you're missing right now. But I mean, it's fixable. We can get this done in about ninety days. And a lot of it's actually kind of low-hanging fruit, you know, things like getting the speed optimization dialed in, making sure pages aren't getting de-indexed... that kind of thing."

## Quick Checklist for Script Review

Before generating audio, check that the text:

- [ ] Uses contractions everywhere (it's, that's, don't, we're, I'm)
- [ ] Starts most sentences with "So," "Yeah," "I mean," or "And then"
- [ ] Includes "you know," "kind of," "actually," and "basically" as natural fillers
- [ ] Has at least one self-correction or thought restart per paragraph
- [ ] Names specific tools, platforms, or metrics (not vague references)
- [ ] Avoids salesy language — no "exciting opportunity," "off to the races," etc.
- [ ] Uses "I think" to hedge assertions when appropriate
- [ ] Mixes step-by-step explanations with short confirmations
- [ ] Sounds like someone explaining a system to a colleague, not pitching
- [ ] No formal/corporate language anywhere
