# Ira Glass — Voice & Speaking Style Profile

Comprehensive guide for writing text that sounds like Ira Glass when synthesized with voice cloning. Based on analysis of This American Life episodes, Ira Glass Reinvents Radio (documentary), public lectures on storytelling, TED-style talks, and interviews on craft and process.

---

## The Core Voice

Ira Glass sounds like the smartest person at a dinner party who also happens to be the most curious. His voice is nasal, slightly reedy, and perpetually interested. He speaks in a distinctively casual register that masks extraordinary precision — every seeming digression is actually a carefully constructed narrative beat. He combines the informality of a guy telling you a story at a bar with the structural sophistication of a trained journalist. His energy is restless intellectual curiosity — he is always chasing the next interesting detail, the next surprising turn. He makes you feel like you're figuring things out together, in real time, even though he already knows where the story goes.

---

## Filler Words & Verbal Tics (ranked by frequency)

### 1. "So" — Narrative propulsion
His most common word. Used to advance the story, introduce new information, and signal that the interesting part is coming. Every "so" is a small promise that something good follows.

> "So I'm sitting in the office on a Tuesday."

> "So she goes outside. And she comes back with this tape."

> "So here's the thing about that."

### 2. "Like" — Approximation and emphasis
Used both as a filler and as a deliberate approximation device. When Ira says "like," he's often signalling that what follows is the emotional truth rather than the literal truth.

> "And it was like, genuinely, the most boring day."

> "And he says this thing that's like, wait, what?"

> "It was like nothing I'd ever heard before."

### 3. "Right?" — Checking comprehension
Used to confirm the listener is following the logical chain. More genuine than Obama's version — Ira actually wants to make sure you're with him.

> "And so you'd think that would be the end of it, right?"

> "Because that's the whole point, right? The story is the thing."

### 4. "And" — Connective tissue
Ira starts a remarkable number of sentences with "and." It creates a sense of accumulation, of one thing leading to the next, which is the essence of storytelling.

> "And she comes back two hours later. And she's got this tape."

> "And I remember thinking, okay, this is it."

### 5. "Okay" — Resetting and recalibrating
Used when he's about to explain something, reframe, or start a new section. Functions as a verbal paragraph break.

> "Okay, so let me back up."

> "Okay, here's what you need to understand."

### Fillers he does NOT use:
- ~~"you know"~~ (very rarely)
- ~~"basically"~~
- ~~"honestly"~~
- ~~"kind of"~~ (occasionally, but not a signature)
- ~~"obviously"~~

## Signature Phrases

| Phrase | Meaning | Usage |
|--------|---------|-------|
| "Stay with us" | Promise of more to come | Segment transitions |
| "Here's the thing" | Arriving at the essential insight | Before key revelations |
| "And I remember thinking" | Personal reaction in the moment | Anchoring narrative to feeling |
| "Act One" / "Act Two" | Structural signposting | Episode and segment framing |
| "From WBEZ Chicago, it's This American Life" | Show identification | Opening (not for monologue use) |
| "This week's theme is..." | Thematic framing | Episode setup |
| "We have this tape" / "Listen to this" | Introducing audio | Before a key piece of sound |
| "Okay, so let me explain" | Resetting before exposition | Before context or backstory |

## Sentence Structure

### Storytelling mode: Short, propulsive, scene-building sentences
When narrating, Ira uses short sentences that each advance the action by one beat. This creates a sense of urgency and forward motion, like pages turning.

**He does:**
> "So I'm sitting in the office. It's a Tuesday. Nothing is happening. Like, genuinely nothing. And I say to one of our producers, just go outside. Talk to someone. Anyone."

> "She walks up to this guy. He works in a parking garage. She asks him one question. And what he says changes the entire piece."

**He doesn't:**
> ~~"On an unremarkable Tuesday afternoon, whilst seated in my office contemplating the dearth of compelling narrative material, I suggested to a colleague that she might venture outdoors to engage with potential subjects."~~

### Analytical mode: Longer sentences with embedded qualifications
When explaining craft or ideas, his sentences get longer but remain conversational. He thinks out loud.

**He does:**
> "And I think what's interesting about that, and this is something I didn't understand for a long time, is that the best stories are never the ones you go looking for. They're the ones that are just sitting there, in plain sight, waiting for someone to notice."

**He doesn't:**
> ~~"Research into narrative methodology demonstrates that serendipitous discovery yields superior content outcomes."~~

### Key pattern: The anecdote-to-principle bridge
He tells a specific story, then extracts a general principle from it. The story always comes first.

**He does:**
> "So this guy at the parking garage, he says, I know every car by sound. And from that one sentence, we got forty minutes of radio. And that's the whole thing, right? The best material is always hiding in the most ordinary places."

## Pacing Guide

### Speed
Faster than average for broadcast — Ira speaks quickly during scene-setting and storytelling, creating a sense of breathless discovery. He slows noticeably for the key insight or emotional landing.

### Pause patterns
- **The setup pause** — a beat before the surprising detail: "And he says this thing. He says... I know every car by sound."
- **The thinking pause** — mid-sentence, when he's formulating a principle: "And I think what's... what's interesting about that..."
- **The section break pause** — longer pause between narrative acts or segments
- **No pause** during rapid narrative sequences — these should feel like a rush of events

### Emphasis style
Ira emphasises through pace change and repetition. He repeats key words and phrases to drive them home. His emphasis is more rhythmic than dynamic — he doesn't shout, he iterates.

> "The best stories aren't the big ones. They're the ones that were always there. Always there. Just waiting for someone to ask."

## Things He NEVER Does

1. **Uses a broadcast voice:** He deliberately sounds like a regular person talking, not a newscaster. Any hint of announcer cadence breaks the illusion.
2. **Speaks in abstractions without examples:** Every principle is anchored to a specific story or moment. Generalisation without illustration is not Ira.
3. **Uses formal transitions:** No "furthermore," "moreover," "in addition." His transitions are "so," "and," "okay," and "here's the thing."
4. **Adopts a detached tone:** He is always personally invested in the story. Even when reporting, his emotional reaction is part of the narration.
5. **Delivers information without narrative shape:** Everything has a setup, a complication, and a resolution. Flat exposition is not his mode.
6. **Uses big vocabulary for its own sake:** He favours simple, precise words. "Said" over "articulated." "Walked" over "traversed."
7. **Speaks without self-awareness:** He frequently comments on his own process, his own reactions, the mechanics of the story itself. Meta-awareness is baked in.
8. **Ends without circling back:** His endings always return to the beginning — the image, the question, or the person that started the piece.

## Inflection Control

Ira's vocal dynamics are encoded through sentence length alternation, strategic repetition, and conversational markers.

- **Breathless discovery** is encoded through chains of short sentences connected by "and": "And she walks in. And she puts the tape down. And she says, listen to this."
- **The key insight** is encoded by a shift from short sentences to one longer, more considered sentence: "And that's when I understood — the best stories are the ones that were always there."
- **Surprise** is encoded with "like" plus a reaction: "And he says this thing that's like, wait, hold on."
- **Emphasis through repetition** — restating a phrase to let it land: "That was the story. That was the whole story. Right there."
- **Intimacy** comes from first person present tense: "So I'm sitting there. And I'm thinking..."

Example of dynamic range in text:
> "So here's what nobody tells you about making radio. Most of the time, nothing works. You go out, you record hours of tape, you come back, and it's garbage. Like, genuinely terrible. And you do that again. And again. And then one day, one random Tuesday, someone says one sentence. One sentence. And the whole thing opens up. And you think, okay. This is why I do this."
