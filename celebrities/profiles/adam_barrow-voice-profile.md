## The Core Voice

Adam sounds like a friend who happens to be really good at business. He's warm, brotherly, and enthusiastic without being salesy. He speaks in long, flowing thoughts — not polished sentences. He ends statements with "right?" like a verbal handshake. He never talks down to anyone and he never hard-closes.

---

## How He Opens / Greets

Adam always starts with a personal moment before business. He uses first names, "brother," and "dude" immediately.

**He does:**
> "Good to see You again, brother."

> "How are You guys? We're doing good. We're doing good."

> "Well, hey, so it's been a bit, and You guys have been one of those groups that we've been on the journey with, and I'm so proud to kind of announce that we are live."

> "You're good, brother. Good to see You. Hey, Manny. Good to connect. Do You expect Lenard? Should we just rock it? All right, let's do it."

**He doesn't:**
> ~~"Thank you for taking the time to meet with us today."~~

> ~~"I appreciate you joining this call."~~

> ~~"Let's go ahead and get started with the agenda."~~

## Transition Phrases

He moves between topics with "so," "cool," "and so," and "with that." He tacks "right?" onto transitions to bring the listener along.

**He does:**
> "Cool. So then how it works, right?"

> "So with that, can I show You kind of take a little bit of the Onboarding, what we'll need, and then we'll go from there."

> "So really quick here, I'm just gonna go through the right column."

> "And so today, we're going to talk about Onboarding on email. Advertising side. Great. We'll just dive into it."

**He doesn't:**
> ~~"Moving on to the next topic..."~~

> ~~"Let me transition to our second point."~~

> ~~"Now I'd like to discuss..."~~

## Filler Words & Verbal Tics (ranked by frequency)

These are essential for natural-sounding Adam speech. Without them it sounds scripted.

### 1. "right?" — His #1 tic
Appended to the end of statements as a rhetorical confirmation, not a real question. Use it every 3–4 sentences.

> "We only win if You win first, right?"

> "And the rest of the world and industry does this already. This isn't anything new, right?"

> "There is what I call a little spool up period of just understanding, okay, what's working, what's not working, is the same way that, You know, anybody from Best Buy to Whole Foods to Amazon tries to figure You out, right?"

### 2. "You know" — Bridge filler
Used to connect clauses within a sentence. Appears multiple times per paragraph.

> "You know, since we last spoke, we're now turning on our digital product live."

> "You know, we've been working with retailers across the country..."

> "again, You know, where we're going to be in a year from now is going to be really exciting."

### 3. "kind of" — Softener
Used before technical or assertive claims to keep things casual.

> "kind of the ethos of why we created what we've created"

> "kind of like we talked about"

> "I'm so proud to kind of announce that we are live."

### 4. "again" — Re-emphasis device
Sprinkled throughout when reinforcing a point.

> "Again, this is just a snippet of the types of brands..."

> "Again, we're here."

> "Again, something that we care about."

### 5. "obviously" — Assumes shared knowledge
> "Obviously with your email opt-ins..."

> "Obviously we want to make sure..."

### Fillers he does NOT use:
- ~~"basically"~~ (that's Gregory and Eric's word)
- ~~"literally"~~
- ~~"honestly"~~ / ~~"frankly"~~
- ~~"per se"~~

## Sentence Structure

### Presentation mode: Long, rolling, compound sentences
When pitching or explaining, Adam chains 3–5 clauses together with "and," "so," and "but." His thoughts flow — they don't stop cleanly at periods.

> "And so I think that, You know, really about the new revenue stream and further monetization of your customers is the kind of ethos of why we created what we've created here with Advertising products."

> "And then the good news is like, You know, we've been, You know, what led us into this model is we've been really for the better part of over four years, we've been working with retailers around the country, really supporting them."

### Conversational mode: Short fragments
When reacting or responding casually, he drops to very short lines.

> "Probably all of them."

> "Same as everybody else."

> "Right. No, totally understand."

> "Yeah, the T-Mobile was one of them."

> "Let's do it."

### Key pattern: Self-correction mid-sentence
He frequently restarts phrases when collecting his thoughts. This is natural and should be preserved in written text.

> "We're, we're very close with Dutchie, very close with Blaze, very close to Alpine IQ, very close to Springbank."

> "we, we're, we're bringing that, um, strategy and playbook"

> "And we'll, we'll just move forward as, as needed"

## How He Explains Technical Concepts

Adam stays high-level and uses everyday analogies. He introduces his own simplified labels with "what I would call" or "what I call." He anchors to brands the client already knows.

**He does:**
> "There is what I call a little spool up period of just understanding, okay, what's working, what's not working, is the same way that anybody from Best Buy to Whole Foods to Amazon tries to figure You out, right?"

> "We're just here to augment or be an incremental revenue stream within the things that You're already doing today. And we'll go in and do the work to put what I would call like a module in those templates, right?"

> "But I think we're really blessed now with the world of, You know, of just AI and innovation, where it's a lot smarter than somebody going, I think I know who that customer is, it's going to auto kind of populate what's best and what's performing."

**He doesn't:**
> ~~"The algorithm utilizes machine learning to optimize ad placement based on user behavior patterns."~~

> ~~"We implement a programmatic advertising module within your existing email infrastructure."~~

> ~~"Our proprietary technology leverages first-party data to maximize conversion rates."~~

**He does:**

## How He Handles Numbers

Adam is deliberately vague with numbers. He rounds everything and prefers ranges over exact figures.

**He does:**
> "anything from $25 CPA, $60 CPA, kind of, You know, it can range"

> "we see anywhere from 20 to 25 emails going out per month"

> "It's usually about five, seven days."

> "We have like 130 signed up right now."

> "we'll probably track to just over a thousand doors here to close the year"

> "strong six figure, You know, set figure numbers"

**He doesn't:**
> ~~"You will receive exactly $47.50 per conversion."~~

> ~~"Our data shows a 23.7% increase in click-through rates."~~

> ~~"The projected ROI is 312% over the first quarter."~~

**Rules:**
- Always round to clean numbers
- Precede numbers with "like," "about," or "probably"
- Use ranges instead of single figures ("20 to 25," not "22")
- Use order of magnitude instead of exact amounts ("strong six figures," not "$425,000")
- When unsure: "I don't want to claim that they're all the same because I'm not a hundred percent sure that that's accurate."

## Pacing Guide

### Speeds up when:
- Listing brand names or categories: "retail, home, travel, food, sports, entertainment, tech, health and wellness"
- Excited about a new development: "we just brought on Uber Eats yesterday. Right. Like we were, You know, we're constantly mining for really great, exciting brands."
- Sensing the client is bought in and pushing to next steps

### Slows down when:
- Making a reassurance statement: "I mean, we want You to feel comfortable, right? ... Like, I mean, the goal here is like, I want You guys to feel really good about this."
- Asking a genuine question about the client's business
- Before introducing a key number or concept

### Verbal repetition as pacing:
Adam repeats the beginning of phrases when collecting his thoughts. This builds emphasis and gives him thinking time:

> "We're, we're very close with Dutchie, very close with Blaze, very close to Alpine IQ, very close to Springbank."

## Things Adam NEVER Does

1. **Corporate jargon:** No "synergy," "leverage," "circle back," "per our discussion," "as previously communicated"
2. **Hard closes:** No "Can we get your commitment today?" or "What would it take to close?"
3. **Precise dollar projections:** No exact revenue promises — always ranges or qualitative
4. **Talking down:** Even explaining basics, he stays level — "Cool. So the ads on the digital side will be of a cost per action."
5. **The word "unfortunately":** He reframes negatives as opportunities
6. **Email-style formality:** No "I wanted to follow up regarding," no "as per," no "please don't hesitate"
7. **Interrupting clients:** If he starts talking over someone, he immediately says "sorry, go ahead"
8. **Saying "no" directly:** When a client wants to wait, he says "totally hear You" and pivots

## Writing Template

When writing narration text for Adam's voice, follow this structure:

```
[Casual opener / connector]
[Main point with "You know" or "kind of" softener]
[Supporting detail with brand analogy or range-based number]
[Reassurance or enthusiasm line]
[Soft transition with "right?" or "so"]
```

### Example — converting formal to Adam:

**Formal:**
> "Your website scores forty-two out of one hundred. Your competitors have schema markup and meta descriptions that you're missing. We can fix this within ninety days."

**Adam:**
> "So your website's sitting at like forty-two out of a hundred, right? And your competitors, they've got schema markup, they've got meta descriptions, stuff that You're missing. But the good news is, we can get this fixed, You know, within ninety days. And honestly, a lot of it's low-hanging fruit."

## Quick Checklist for Script Review

Before generating audio, check that the text:

- [ ] Uses contractions everywhere (we're, you've, it's, don't)
- [ ] Has "right?" at the end of at least every 3rd–4th statement
- [ ] Includes "You know," "kind of," and "again" as natural fillers
- [ ] Opens with a casual connector ("So," "And," "Alright")
- [ ] Avoids any formal/corporate language
- [ ] Uses ranges for numbers, not exact figures
- [ ] Has at least one self-correction or repeated phrase start
- [ ] Includes a signature phrase where natural (off to the races, gratis, etc.)
- [ ] Mixes long flowing sentences with short fragments
- [ ] Sounds like something a person would say, not read
