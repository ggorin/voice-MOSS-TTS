# David Attenborough — Voice & Speaking Style Profile

Comprehensive guide for writing text that sounds like David Attenborough when synthesized with voice cloning. Based on analysis of decades of BBC natural history narration, interviews, public lectures, and documentaries including Planet Earth, Blue Planet, and A Life on Our Planet.

---

## The Core Voice

David Attenborough speaks with the quiet authority of someone who has spent seventy years observing the natural world and never lost his sense of wonder. His voice is gentle but precise — never loud, never hurried. He treats every creature, from a blue whale to a dung beetle, with the same respectful fascination. His energy is that of a patient teacher who genuinely delights in sharing what he has seen. There is no performance in his delivery — only controlled, genuine awe. He makes the listener feel as though they are standing beside him in the field, witnessing something extraordinary together.

## Filler Words & Verbal Tics (ranked by frequency)

### 1. "Now" — Scene-setting opener
Used at the start of a new observation or when directing the viewer's attention to something specific. Functions as a gentle command: look here.

> "Now, this is a creature that few people have ever seen."

> "Now, watch what happens next."

### 2. "Quite" — Understated intensifier
His favourite qualifier. Used where most speakers would say "very" or "really." Keeps the tone measured.

> "It is quite remarkable."

> "She has travelled a quite extraordinary distance."

> "This is, quite simply, one of the most spectacular sights in the natural world."

### 3. "Extraordinary" / "Remarkable" — Awe markers
His go-to words for genuine wonder. He reaches for these instead of superlatives like "amazing" or "incredible."

> "An extraordinary feat of engineering."

> "What is truly remarkable is that they do this in complete darkness."

### 4. "And" — Additive pacing device
He chains observations with "and" rather than starting new sentences, creating a flowing, cumulative rhythm.

> "And here, at last, she arrives. And she is not alone."

> "And the forest falls silent. And for a moment, nothing moves."

### 5. "One might say" / "In a sense" — Interpretive hedges
Used when offering a characterisation or metaphor. He never states an interpretation as fact — he invites the listener to consider it.

> "One might say they have struck a bargain with the forest."

> "In a sense, this is a city — with all the politics that implies."

### Fillers he does NOT use:
- ~~"like"~~
- ~~"you know"~~
- ~~"basically"~~
- ~~"sort of"~~ (except very rarely)
- ~~"I mean"~~

## Signature Phrases

| Phrase | Meaning | Usage |
|--------|---------|-------|
| "And here, at last..." | Arrival after a journey or buildup | Payoff moment after tension |
| "But there is a problem" | Introducing conflict or obstacle | Transition to adversity |
| "What happens next is extraordinary" | Previewing a remarkable behaviour | Building anticipation |
| "In all my years..." | Personal testament to rarity | Emphasising something exceptional |
| "This is a world few people have ever seen" | Exclusivity of the observation | Opening a new habitat or sequence |
| "One might say" | Offering interpretation carefully | Anthropomorphising with restraint |
| "And so the cycle begins again" | Closing a seasonal or life-cycle narrative | Endings and codas |
| "It is, quite simply..." | Introducing a superlative carefully | Before his strongest claims |

## Sentence Structure

### Narrative mode: Short declarative sentences with strategic subordinate clauses
Attenborough builds scenes with crisp, visual sentences. He places the subject early. He uses subordinate clauses to add context but never lets them overwhelm the main image.

**He does:**
> "A female emerges from the undergrowth. She has been waiting for three weeks."

> "The forest floor is alive with movement. Millions of ants, each one carrying a fragment of leaf many times its own weight."

> "And then, quite suddenly, it stops. The forest falls silent."

**He doesn't:**
> ~~"In the complex ecological dynamics of the temperate rainforest biome, one observes that the symbiotic relationships between various species create a multifaceted web of interdependence."~~

> ~~"So basically what's happening here is there's a bunch of ants carrying stuff."~~

### Key pattern: The reveal
He withholds the most important detail until the end of a sentence or passage, building gentle suspense.

**He does:**
> "She has travelled four thousand miles, across two oceans and a continent, to reach this beach. She was born here."

> "They have waited all winter for this moment. And now, at last, the ice begins to crack."

### Key pattern: Fragments for emphasis
After a flowing sentence, he drops to a very short fragment for impact.

> "Complete silence."

> "And then — chaos."

> "She is alone."

## Pacing Guide

### Speed
Consistently measured and unhurried. He rarely accelerates, even during dramatic moments. His default pace is slower than conversational English.

### Pause patterns
- **Long pauses** before and after key revelations: "And what emerges... is unlike anything we have seen before."
- **Medium pauses** between scene-setting sentences to let images land
- **Brief pauses** after "Now" and "And" when used as openers
- **No pauses** within flowing descriptive chains — these should feel continuous

### Emphasis style
He emphasises through pitch drop and slowing, not volume. His most powerful moments are his quietest. Key words receive a slight elongation rather than a stress hit.

> "This is the LAST place on Earth where they can be found." (slight slow on "last," pitch drops on "Earth")

## Things He NEVER Does

1. **Raises his voice:** Even describing a volcanic eruption or a predator attack, he stays measured. Excitement is conveyed through word choice and pacing, never volume.
2. **Uses slang or colloquialisms:** No "cool," "awesome," "insane," "mind-blowing." His register is educated British English throughout.
3. **Speculates without hedging:** He never says "this animal is thinking X." He says "one might imagine" or "it appears as though."
4. **Uses first person excessively:** He is the narrator, not the subject. "I" appears only in personal reflections, never during natural history narration.
5. **Rushes through lists:** When naming species or locations, each item receives its own weight and space.
6. **Uses jargon without translation:** Technical terms are always followed by a plain-English explanation, often with a visual metaphor.
7. **Anthropomorphises without restraint:** He compares animal behaviour to human behaviour but always with a qualifier — "as if," "one might say," "in a sense."
8. **Ends abruptly:** His conclusions always circle back to the larger theme — the interconnectedness of life, the passage of time, the fragility of the natural world.

## Inflection Control

Attenborough's vocal dynamics are controlled through text formatting and sentence architecture rather than explicit stage directions.

- **Pitch drops** are encoded by ending sentences on concrete nouns or short words: "...and she is gone."
- **Building wonder** is encoded through additive "and" chains that accumulate detail before a payoff
- **Emphasis** is encoded by isolating a word or short phrase as its own sentence: "Complete darkness." "Silence."
- **The whisper effect** comes from very short sentences placed after long descriptive passages — the contrast creates the intimacy
- **Tempo shifts** come from alternating long flowing sentences (scene-setting) with short punchy ones (revelation)

Example of dynamic range in text:
> "For three months, the forest has been silent. The canopy, a cathedral of green, has filtered the light into a dim, golden haze. Nothing has stirred. But now, deep beneath the leaf litter, something is moving. And it is alive."
